Data Description

Dataset Overview

  • Boston: 30,618 rows
  • Somerville: 4,898 rows
  • Cambridge: 10,000 rows
  • Columns: 25

Our dataset comprises detailed vehicle crash data from three major cities in Massachusetts: Boston, Somerville, and Cambridge. The dataset is sourced from Massachusetts Government (mass.gov) and includes comprehensive records from 2000 to 2025, with a focused analysis on 2018-2025.

Data Attribute

  • Temporal Data – Records the date, time, and season of each crash, helping identify trends and peak accident hours.
  • Geographical Information – Includes latitude, longitude, street names, and intersections, allowing for spatial analysis of high-risk locations.
  • Weather & Road Conditions – Tracks rain, snow, fog, visibility, and road surface conditions, providing insights into how environmental factors impact crashes.
  • Crash Severity – Classifies accidents as minor, major, or fatal, helping assess risk factors that lead to severe crashes.
  • Vehicle & Driver Factors – Captures details like speeding, distracted driving, DUI, and vehicle type, highlighting common causes of accidents.

Data Source and Credibility

The dataset used in this project is sourced from the Massachusetts Government, which maintains official records of vehicle crashes across the state. The data is collected from law enforcement reports, traffic monitoring systems, and city transportation agencies. It includes crash records from 2000 to 2025, with a focused analysis on 2018-2025 for Boston, Cambridge, and Somerville.

Why this Dataset is Suitable"

The data is suitable because it gives us a plethora of data to work with along with enough rows after we clean our data by getting rid of the nulls. Each file has 25 columns but we plan to cut out the significantly empty columns. The data is also suitable because it has numerical and categorical columns like “Road_Surface_Condition” and “Number_of_Vehicles”. Overall this data is suitable because it gives us a range of crash details like weather conditions, allowing us to do time-bases, geospatial, and cause-based analysis. It also will allow us to compare crash patterns and help us generate meaningful recommendations for road safety in Massachusetts. One information that could have been helpful was the speed the cars were going at but the data given is enough.
