Summary of Findings

Key Insights

  • Afternoons have the highest crash frequency, followed by mornings — likely due to traffic volume during commuting hours.
  • Cambridge has the highest crash rate per 10,000 residents, suggesting a greater density of incidents relative to population.
  • Angle and rear-end collisions are among the most reported, often resulting in non-fatal injuries.
  • The severity of crashes varies across cities, with Boston having the highest count of serious injuries overall.

Conclusions

  • High crash volumes during afternoon hours suggest that targeted traffic management during peak commuting periods could significantly reduce incidents.
  • Cambridge’s high crash rate per capita highlights the need for tailored traffic safety measures in densely populated urban areas, regardless of city size.
  • Most crashes are non-fatal but often involve rear-end or angle collisions, indicating opportunities to improve road design, signage, and driver awareness campaigns.

Recommendations

  • Enhance traffic control measures during afternoon peak hours, such as adjusting signal timing and increasing enforcement in high-volume areas.
  • Target Cambridge with customized safety strategies, including awareness campaigns and infrastructure updates to reduce per-capita crash rates.
  • Improve signage and road design at intersections prone to angle or rear-end collisions to reduce their frequency and severity.

Future Directions

  • Incorporate vehicle and driver data (e.g., vehicle type, driver age, license history) to better understand causes of crashes.
  • Analyze crash trends over time using yearly breakdowns to detect long-term changes and policy impacts.
  • Use predictive modeling (e.g., machine learning) to identify high-risk zones and forecast future crash likelihoods.
  • Include road condition data like lighting, surface quality, and signage visibility to explore environmental risk factors more deeply.
